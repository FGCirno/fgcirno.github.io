My Favorite 2025 Things!

Today, I wanted to share with you some of my favorite 2025 releases. There was so much I loved that came out this year, and I simply want to share lots of it with you!

Hollow Knight: Silksong

Let's get the obvious one out of the way right now. Silksong was every bit as amazing as anyone who was hyped for it expected. I'm a somewhat new fan to Hollow Knight. I bought the first game when it first launched on Steam, but put off playing it until Silksong's release date got announced. In those two weeks between Silksong getting it's release date announced and coming out, I played through the entire first game in 40hrs.

The original game had some pretty clear flaws to me. Many lamented its map while I found its gameplay loop pretty simplistic. Silksong in many ways improves on what Hollow Knight did, and more faithfully creates Team Cherry's vision with the first game. It is the idea of what the first game wanted to be turned into an actual game.

Of course all aspects of Silksong I found wonderfully melancholic and enjoyable, but what surprised me the most about the game was the pacing. It being split into two major acts, Act 1: Pharloom and Act 2: Citadel, it basically feels like Hollow Knight got two different sequel games. While there is a narrative throughline with Hornet immediately wanting to rise to the top of the Citadel from Pharloom's Bone Bottom, there's a clear shift between the first and second halves.

Basically, the Pharloom section of the game feels much the same as the original game. You're exploring new areas which are very endemic, lots of caves and fields that make up the world, where as the second act completely turns that on it's head. Being entirely inside the Citadel, it's much more like you're stuck in something like Dracula's Castle. In that sense, it really feels like the true game starts when you're half way through the game, at the Citadel's gates.

Yet the whole game throughout is great. There's a lot of narrative throughlines, with the earlier parts of the game setting up the religious capitalistic civilization these bugs in Pharloom are victim to and that theme of corrupting capital remains throughout the entire game. Similarly, this game has a big focus on it's soundtrack, and songs that are important to the heart of the Citadel play as simply echos at the far bottom below it, faintly heard, missed if you're not aware.

There's so much to love about Silksong, no matter what you're there for. Even if you don't care about story, music or world building like I've talked about the game still has fantastic punishing but fair gameplay that's way, way faster than the first game. There's a lot to love with Silksong, and it's easily a near perfect game in my eyes.

City: The Animation

City is the sequel series to Kyoto Animation's 2011 comedy classic Nichijou and having that information might paint some assumptions in your mind. You'll do best to dismiss them, because City is ultimately it's own thing. It does what it's name implies, and tries to tell a heartful slice of life story where the city itself is the protagonist. You'll find an intermingling cast of wholly interesting characters who have hardly anything to do with each other.

There's the Makabe family and their "Western Food Restraunt" (which exclusively serves Japanese food every time we see them), and then there's Nagumo, the closest thing we have to a main character in this series, who happens to work part time for them. Nagumo is connected to her roommates Niikura and Wako Izumi. Wako has a sister named Riko who is in middle school, and through her we get to meet an entire class worth of characters. This goes on and on, every character in City is connected in some incredibly minor way and it leads to really fun little intertwined moments that really work. It's easily the best "City is the Protagonist" series I've seen, and succeeds at that gimmick better than anything I've ever seen before.

City definitely wears its heart on its sleeve, yet it's not just wholesome. While it's genuine moments, characters and locale are why I adore it, it is also probably the single most impressive piece of animation to come out this year. There are plenty of moment of sakuga in every single episode, used often to emphasize punchlines like in Nichijou but often times there's moments that are just highly animated for no good reason. It's fantastic and an absolutely delight to the eyes.

Every time there's a big pop off in the animation, it's followed by some sort of emotional resonance. Some character feeling pure rage, bliss, desparation, embarassment. Basically, the sakuga is there in support of the heart of the show more than punchlines, which really makes you relate to the characters even more.

Perhaps the peak of City's amazing animation would have to be it's incredibly impressive Episode 5. In this episode, Nagumo has been kidnapped by a perhaps too kind butler who wishes to reward her with a feast and an award for a good deed that she doesn't even remember doing. While she tries to escape from the "Hospitality Tower" by conquering the 12 floors below the one shes on, we slowly pull the camera back and start focusing on other characters. First, we see Niikura looking for her pendant (which she lost in the previous episode) while animations of Nagumo and her party try to escampe the tower plays on the right, then we get to see the Go Home Club added to the mix, and the middle schools soccer team and so on.

Eventually, we're left with a screen that looks like this:

You're not meant to focus on everything at once, more so it gives you so much more perspective the next time you watch the episode. I've seen it five times through showing friends the series and every single time I learn something new. Even in just this gif there's so much detail that, since all the secondary stories are silent, you won't have full answers to til later. We're introduced to characters in this segment just through them having a little story in their own box in this episode that wouldn't get named let alone properly introduced until the next episode, or episode 7, episode 8.

All of this has a purpose as well. We learn that these aren't separate stories. All these people are going to the same place. This is truly a *City* animation. A bustling, living and breathing society where everyone is moving within and doing their own thing. It's kind of the thesis statement for why I find the show so good in general, compounded into a single incredibly well animated episode. It's fantastic, and so is the rest of the series.

Sunshine Backwards

Ooh, this one is REALLY painful.

In so many ways, Sunshine Backwards just feels like my history being projected onto my monitor. It's a story about a fuck up 20 something trans girl who comes home from her dead end job and just puts on old Adult Swim recordings from the early 2000s on to just sit there, and not feel anything. Getting as crossfaded as possible. Her past is incredibly messy, and we learn that she's been severely wronged by many people in her life. She feels like she was groomed, and when her groomer came out as trans after her she doesn't think they're valid. Just... it's so fucking close to what I've lived through. It's like the characters of Sonia and Rosy are conjoined fragments of everyone I ever knew growing up and myself, and the gruesome and horrific way the game depicts Sonia's trauma is potent as someone who can relate.

Another thing Sonia is nostalgic for is the time when she was obsessed with Sonic the Hedgehog creepypasta. This game is called Sunshine Backwards afterall, so of course we're gonna talk about playing Can You Feel The Sunshine on reverse while in your bathroom to try and summon the Tails Doll to come and kill you. Maybe about the only difference between my experience and Sonia's is that when I did that with my childhood friend I didn't wet myself or get sent home. But, I have a very, very strong memory of me and my friend being so obnoxious it work up her dad, and he was NOT happy.

There's this other memory I have that to this day I have no idea what to make of. I was alone at night, probably 6 or 7 years old. I see the pulsing of a red light outside my window, focused around a diamond shape like the Tails Doll's gemstone. It stays still for a while, before getting closer and closer rapidly. I'm terrified, I stop looking out of the window and just lay as still as I can in my bed... ... ... and then, out of nowhere, something falls on top of me. Something soft, but with immense weight. I throw it as hard as I can and run to my Great Grandmother who is four rooms away on the other side of the house and just cry and cry while hugging her. I seriously have no idea what could've fallen on me, and that memory hasn't left me since.

My experiences with the Tails Doll were so important to me, and I was so mistified by the curse and how cool and edgy I thought the internets perception of the character was, that I made a lot of my early usernames TailsDoll98. I was completely enamored with the little guy and even now I hold some fondness for that overly edgy period of my life. That alone would make me on board with Sunshine Backwards, and yet it also ended up being deeply relatable for reasons separate from that little Sonic character.

I don't know if myself and the VN's creator Clover sharing an interest in the Tails Doll at formative points in our lives just made us have similar life experiences, but I'm incredibly happy to know someone shared so much of the experience that make me who I am today. I'm happy, but it also makes me feel complex. If part of this VN are as real as Clover implies, then I'm sorry for all she had to go through. Like I said, I personally know so much of the hardship that goes on in this visual novel. Meeting an internet stranger who gets very, very close to you who happens to be 10 years older than you when you're a teenager, the feeling years later that meeting *them* was the single worst thing to happen to you and is a traceable point where your life derailed for good, complete and utter burnout with the modernity of life and having to fucking work at a place where nobody gives a shit about you, rambling on and on so passionately while nobody has ANY sympathy for whats happened to you. I get it, and I also get that so much of what happens in this VN is pure venting. Being able to take all that and be like "well, what *if* I snapped. Lets write a scenario about that. What if, in my dying dream I consumed the one who completely ruined my life." It's pure catharsis.

Elden Ring Nightreign

Elden Ring Nightreign in my opinion is complete lightning in a bottle. They took all of Elden Ring's bosses, themes, the worldbuilding concepts and just condensed it into a completely different experience. Nightreign in its purest essence embodies Elden Ring: Arcade Mode. It is short, spontaneous gameplay segmented into 40 minute play sessions instead of being a full length RPG like the base game, and it couldn't be more beautiful.

With mechanics inspired by battle royale games like Fortnite with it's encroaching circle and random loot, Nightreign always keeps you moving and on your toes. The game wants to make sure you hardly have any time to think about what you're doing as you go from boss to boss, constantly moving and fighting to prepare for the encroaching night. It is pure gameplay bliss, and probably one of the best uses of reusing assets to make an ENTIRELY different experience out there. Everyone knows Elden Ring's bosses. So many of these bosses were very famous for their difficulty, movesets, their gimmicks. But for me, who only played through the whole thing one time, Nightreign has been perfect to really ingrain these characters, these bosses and their movesets into my thick skull. I never knew how truly special everything to do with these bosses were til I played an upward of a hundred runs in Nightreign. Now I truly know these bosses

With the Deep of Night gamemode added this last September and promises of future DLC on the way, it seems like Nightreign will be supported for a long time still. With Deep of Night, there's a really good progression where each difficulty mode feels *just* hard enough for whatever skill level you're at, normal < everdark < deep of night. I think what I'm getting at is I feel there's so much intentionality with Nightreign's game design.

The 8bitdo Pro 3 Controller

Lets finish with a few simple ones. Controller Manufacturer 8bitdo put out a new model of their Pro series controller the Pro 3 this year, and it has quickly risen to be one of my favorite controllers of all time. It's very tacticle, there's a lot of feedback when you press buttons (notably the d-pad is incredibly clicky, as are R2 and L2 when you lock them and the new R4 and L4 buttons on the shoulders using actual mouse parts for maximum auditory and physical response).

The feel of the controller much like the Pro 2 is very grainy when unboxed, but it's almost guaratneed that this grain goes away after months of use (mine certainly has). It's unfortunate because I really like how the rough feel of the controller is before that gets worn down. The general design of the controller is very similar to either the Dualshock 4 or more directly the Wii Classic Controller Pro (itself being inspired by the SNES controller). 8bitdo often makes controllers that imitate other controllers, like their SN30 which is VERY clearly an SNES controller. The Pro 3, while evocative of other designs (even having the same face buttons as an SNES controller, you can swap them out for those if you really wanted to) it simply feels so much better than any other controller I've used aside from maybe the Dualsense.

Great controller, almost no notes. My few problems come with the controllers gimmicks. It has easily interchangable face buttons and because of that, they're built more brittle than they would be otherwise. My biggest problem comes with the lack of six-axis gyro while using the controllers dongle. The recent Windows 11 update at the start of the year completely broke my bluetooth adapter, so I'm completely out of luck in terms of being able to use gyro at all with this controller on PC. I was hoping they'd fix that over the Pro 2, but they let me down.
